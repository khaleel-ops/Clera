Opening

Trust Is the Interface

Clera isn’t just matching talent to companies — it’s shaping the emotional experience between introduction and opportunity.

I’m a product designer. I think Clera is one of the few AI products with a soul — a real point of view about how working life should feel. I’d like to spend two weeks helping you ship one focused improvement to the talent experience, on a fixed fee, with zero commitment after. The rest of this page explains what I see, what I’d work on, and why I’m a good bet.

What I see

Three things that stood out

Reading the manifesto and using the chat, three things became obvious about your product.

  • Trust

    The real product is trust, not matching. Talent are handing over the most personal data they own — their ambition. The interface has to feel like an advocate, not surveillance.

  • Chat

    The chat is doing two jobs at once: capturing high-signal preferences and being the first impression of “what kind of agent is this.” Most chat-first products only design for the first.

  • Magic

    The magic moment lives outside your app. The Slack message to a hiring manager. The “intro accepted” notification. The eight quiet days before an interview. That’s where Clera either feels like a friend or feels like a recruiter.

Hypotheses

Three places I'd start

Outside reads, organized by where I'd put the design effort first. All three sit on the talent side — exactly the surface the sprint covers.

01

Intake is where taste shows up

It’s where match quality is set, where trust is won or lost in the first 90 seconds, and where you signal “agent on your side” vs “another recruiter chatbot.” A good intake should feel like a friend with great instincts — one that knows when to dig in, when to skip ahead, and when to not ask another question.

Design lever: tone-of-voice, question pacing, “permission to skip” affordances, what’s shown in the silence between messages.

02

The middle of the wait

Eight days to first interview is fast. But what happens on days two through seven? If the talent feels like they’re back on a job board — refreshing, wondering — Clera collapses into the same anxiety it set out to kill.

Design lever: proactive status, named context (“Daniel at Casca is reading your profile this morning”), a dashboard that earns a reopen instead of asking for one.

03

Reasoning in human language

Recruiters get away with opaque pitches because users have no alternative. An agent shouldn’t. If Clera shows the reasoning behind every introduction — in human language, not LLM hedge-speak — talent stop second-guessing and start trusting the agent’s taste.

Design lever: a “match memo” surface — short, specific, written like a friend would write it. Used on both sides of the marketplace.

Two more worth a conversation but outside this sprint's scope: the hiring-manager surface (your revenue ceiling) and the free tools as pre-signup first impressions. Happy to dig into either on the call.

How I work

Principles for the sprint

  • Looms over meetings. Written memos over slide decks. I respect that you raised pre-seed money to ship, not to sit in design reviews.
  • I won’t open Figma until we agree on the user problem. Pretty pixels in service of a wrong hypothesis is the most expensive design output there is.
  • I design with your stack in mind — handoff specs, component thinking, real states (loading, empty, error, edge), and prose your dev team can ship from.
  • I treat your brand like it’s mine. The manifesto’s tone — confident, anti-fluff, warm — shows up in every microcopy choice, not just the marketing site.
